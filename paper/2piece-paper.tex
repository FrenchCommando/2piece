\begin{abstract}
Short-maturity expansion of implied volatility in local volatility
models is well-documented for smooth local volatility functions:
\citet{berestycki2002} for order zero, \citet{henrylabordere2008}
for $O(T)$, and \citet{gatheral2012} for $O(T^{2})$. The non-smooth
case has been investigated by \citet{costeanuPirjol2011}, who derive
the non-analytic $\sqrt{T}$ term for a $C^{0}$ knot at the forward
and tabulate the higher powers their framework admits 
depending on the degree of the discontinuity. 
This paper gives the leading non-analytic correction in closed form
for a specific case --- time-homogeneous cubic local volatility with
one $C^{2}$ knot at the forward: a $T^{3/2}$ term, validated
empirically against Dupire's forward PDE.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Vanilla options on US indexes like SPX are liquid for a wide range
of strikes and maturities. These options are European, and
practitioners routinely use Black--Scholes to convert prices to
implied volatilities.

For a single maturity, it is common to fit a curve to capture the
implied volatilities across strikes. Methods vary by use case.

The method I am interested in is based on Berestycki--Busca--Florent
(BBF) \citep{berestycki2002}; its use as an implied-volatility
parametrization has practitioner precedent in Hagan and Woodward's
equivalent Black volatilities \citep{haganWoodward1999}. The main
idea is to assume that the local volatility is a parametric function
and use an analytic formula to map it to an implied-volatility curve.
It is important to note that this is just a tool: I do not imply
that the dynamics of the volatility surface follow that
local-volatility function, the same way I do not assume that
volatility is constant when using the Black--Scholes formula. BBF
show that the leading-order mapping is just a harmonic mean of
$\sigma_{\mathrm{loc}}$. The simplicity of the formula made it
attractive for practitioners, and De~Marco \citep{demarco2021} gives
the structural characterization of when (and why) an
implied-volatility smile admits such a harmonic-mean representation.

Since BBF is a zeroth-order expansion, it is natural to look at the
next orders to make the mapping more accurate: Henry-Labord\`ere
(PHL1) \citep{henrylabordere2008} adds the $\sigma_1$ heat-kernel
term, and Gatheral, Hsu, Laurence, Ouyang and Wang (GHLOW2)
\citep{gatheral2012} add the $\sigma_2$ term on top. Higher-order
accuracy does not make BBF worse for its main use case --- fitting a
single smile --- but it matters when fine-tuning an initial guess
inside a local-volatility calibration loop, where the map's accuracy
propagates into optimizer quality, and for \emph{arbitrage
reassurance}: since BBF is only leading order, a BBF-fitted smile
can carry sub-basis-point static no-arbitrage violations (butterfly,
calendar) that PHL1 closes by sitting much closer to the true Dupire
IV.

However, the higher-order maps degrade the regularity that the
implied volatility inherits from the local-volatility input. The
$\sigma_1$ expansion underlying PHL1 (and hence GHLOW2) is derived
assuming $\sigma_{\mathrm{loc}}\in C^3$. A piecewise-cubic local
volatility --- the natural parametric choice for shape flexibility
--- is only $C^2$ at its knots (only $\sigma_{\mathrm{loc}}'''$ jumps
there), so PHL1 develops a localized residual at the knot strike.
BBF, a harmonic-mean integral, is insensitive to the same regularity
gap and stays smooth across the knot.

The analogous problem one regularity class harsher was studied by
Costeanu and Pirjol \citep{costeanuPirjol2011} in normal (Bachelier)
implied vol: a $C^0$ local vol with a jump in
$\sigma'_{\mathrm{loc}}$ at the forward, treated as a first-order
perturbation in the Duhamel/Dyson expansion of the Dupire
call-price evolution against constant-$\sigma$ Black. They prove
that the ATM normal IV picks up a non-analytic $\sqrt{T}$ correction
with universal coefficient $\tfrac{1}{16}\sqrt{\pi/2}\approx 0.0784$
proportional to the jump, and their Table~5.1 tabulates the higher
powers of $T$ the framework admits, depending on the degree of the
discontinuity. This paper uses the same
technique to derive the analogous correction for a $C^2$
piecewise-cubic local vol with the knot at the forward (only
$\sigma'''_{\mathrm{loc}}$ jumps), in log-normal Black IV: a
non-analytic $T^{3/2}$ term in closed form, fitting the $T^{3/2}$
slot of their Table~5.1 row $n=1$. I compare the corrected mappings
against the Dupire forward PDE \citep{dupire1994} on parameters
calibrated from real SPXW surfaces.

\paragraph{Compute cost.} The Dupire forward PDE is the ground
truth, but it has no closed form; the asymptotic-map candidates
considered here trade some accuracy for closed-form evaluation.

\paragraph{Extrapolation.} BBF can in principle be tuned so the
wings respect Lee's moment formula \citep{lee2004}, the universal
asymptotic bound on the deep-wing slope of any arbitrage-free smile.
Wings are outside the scope of this paper; the focus is the ATM
correction.


\section{Related work}

The map from a parametric local-vol surface to implied vol through a
small-time asymptotic is one strand of a wider literature on parametric
volatility surfaces. The direct predecessor for the knot-residual
question, the broader asymptotic-map literature, and the orthogonal
direct-IV parametrization literature are taken in turn.

\paragraph{Direct prior art.} Costeanu and Pirjol
\citep{costeanuPirjol2011} (introduced above) and this paper share
the same Duhamel-perturbation framework; the two universal
coefficients sit in adjacent slots of the same Table~5.1 ---
$\tfrac{1}{16}\sqrt{\pi/2}\approx 0.0784$ for their $C^{0}$
$\sqrt{T}$ correction and $3\sqrt{2\pi}/128\approx 0.0588$ derived
in \S\ref{sec:universal} for this paper's $C^{2}$ $T^{3/2}$
correction. The kernel $K_1(x,0)$ of \S\ref{sec:universal} is the
corresponding first-order Duhamel kernel for the local-volatility
perturbation; in its bridge form it is a one-dimensional integral
over $\lambda\in[0,1]$ whose integrand is the conditional expectation
of the source along a standard Brownian bridge.

\paragraph{Higher-order LV-to-IV asymptotics.} The BBF/PHL1/GHLOW2
ladder above derives from the heat-kernel expansion of the local-vol
transition density; Berestycki et al.\ extended the same heat-kernel
approach to stochastic volatility in \citep{berestycki2004sv}, and
Paulot \citep{paulot2009} derives the second-order expansion on the
stochastic-volatility side, with SABR as the worked example. A parallel
methodology, due to Pagliarani--Pascucci \citep{pagliarani2012} and
extended to local--stochastic models by Lorig--Pagliarani--Pascucci
\citep{lorig2017}, derives implied-vol
expansions by Taylor-expanding the diffusion coefficients about a
point where they are assumed smooth; the knot case here is non-smooth
at the natural expansion point and lies outside that scope. A further
parallel route due to Medvedev and Scaillet
\citep{medvedevScaillet2007} writes the implied-vol PDE in
moneyness-degree $\theta=k/(\sigma\sqrt T)$ and solves an ODE in
$\theta$ at each order in $\sqrt T$; the polynomial-in-$\theta$
solution class assumed there is broken by the non-smooth forcing a
$C^2$ knot would inject into the same ODE. A
quadrature-based alternative due to Guyon and Henry-Labord\`ere
\citep{guyonHenryLabordere2010} sidesteps the smoothness assumption
entirely --- their heat-kernel estimator writes $\siv^2$ as a
weighted space--time average of $\sloc^2$ evaluated at quadrature
points, so a $C^2$ knot is handled by construction with no
correction term --- at the cost of a 2D quadrature per strike and
no closed-form $\siv(k;\theta)$ for an optimizer to differentiate.
De~Marco's harmonic-mean characterization \citep{demarco2021} pins
what the leading-order map is approximating, and hence what the
next-order gap (the object this paper's correction closes) actually
is.

\paragraph{Direct implied-vol parametrizations.} The other side of the
design choice is to parametrize the implied-vol surface directly. SABR
\citep{hagan2002} fits a leading-order asymptotic of a stochastic-vol
model. SVI \citep{gatheral2004svi} and its arbitrage-free refinement
SSVI \citep{gatheralJacquier2014} drop the dynamic model and write the
smile itself as a parametric form, then constrain the parameters to
enforce static no-arbitrage. Andreasen--Huge \citep{andreasenHuge2011}
fits a piecewise-constant local vol in one implicit-Euler step of the
Dupire PDE; the resulting prices are arbitrage-free in the sense of the
discretized scheme, with as many vol pieces as quotes. This is a
different design point from this paper's: the local vol is piecewise-constant
so it carries no smooth parametric derivatives for an optimizer to
follow, the calibration carries a numerical scheme inside it (there
is no closed-form $\siv(k;\theta)$), and ``arbitrage-free'' is the
one-step finite-difference notion rather than the continuous Dupire
model's. Itkin and Lipton \citep{itkinLipton2018} sharpen the
analytical thread of this family by fitting a piecewise-linear local
variance via Laplace transform in time and Kummer hypergeometric
functions in space, which removes the FD-discretization step but
keeps the piecewise low-order parametric structure and the lack of a
closed-form $\siv(k;\theta)$. The
parametric-LV-through-PHL1 route trades that for a closed-form
asymptotic on the local-vol side, with arbitrage reassurance carried
by PHL1's tightness against the true Dupire implied vol.

\section{Setup}

\subsection{Local-volatility parametrization}\label{sec:setup-lv}

Work in log-moneyness $k=\log(K/F)$, forward measure ($F=1$, $r=0$, so
the underlying is driftless and there are no rates or dividends to
discount), single maturity. The local volatility is time-homogeneous and cubic in
log-moneyness, in annualized \% units:
\begin{equation}
\sloc(k) = \sigma + \beta k + \alpha k^2 + \gamma k^3 .
\end{equation}
A knot at the money adds a one-sided jump $\delta$ in the cubic coefficient,
\begin{equation}
\sloc(k) = \sigma + \beta k + \alpha k^2 + \gamma k^3
           + \delta\,k^3\,\Hev(k) ,
\label{eq:perturb}
\end{equation}
with $\Hev$ the Heaviside step. Because $k^3$ and its first two derivatives
vanish at $k=0$, \eqref{eq:perturb} is $C^2$ at the knot; only $\sloc'''$
jumps by $\delta$. This is precisely the structure a piecewise-cubic
calibrator produces, with the knot moved to ATM.

I use the Black $T=1$ convention; $\mathrm{scale}=\sqrt{252/n}\cdot 100$
maps total vol to annualized \% for $n=\max(1,\mathrm{DTE})$ business
days. Each cubic coefficient is divided by $\mathrm{scale}$ when it
enters an asymptotic-map formula and multiplied back at the output.

\paragraph{Units.} Two regimes throughout the paper. Anything displayed
--- the cubic definition above, figures, error tables --- is in
annualized \%. Anything inside an asymptotic-map formula --- BBF0,
$\sigma_1$, $\sigma_2$, the kernels $K_1$ / $K_1^{\mathrm{dir}}$, the
extension piece, the knot correction --- is in total-vol units.
The scale factor above converts at the boundary; within either regime
the symbols $\sigma,\beta,\alpha,\gamma,\delta$ denote that regime's
numerical values. This matches practitioner convention: plotting is in
annualized vols, numerical methods are in total-vol.

\subsection{The asymptotic maps}\label{sec:setup-maps}

\paragraph{Notation.} Let $\siv(k)$ denote the Black--Scholes implied
volatility recovered from the undiscounted call price $C(T,k)$ that
the local-vol model \eqref{eq:perturb} generates via the Dupire
forward equation; this is the ground-truth implied vol that every
asymptotic map in this paper approximates, and the $\siv$ that
appears in eq.~\eqref{eq:expansion} below. The small-$T$ heat-kernel
expansion of the local-vol transition density
(Berestycki--Busca--Florent \citep{berestycki2002},
Henry-Labord\`ere \citep{henrylabordere2008}, Gatheral et al.\
\citep{gatheral2012}) yields a closed-form expansion $\siv \approx
\sigma_0 + \sigma_1\,T + \sigma_2\,T^{2} + \cdots$, with each
$\sigma_n$ a closed-form functional of $\sloc$. Under the $T=1$
convention (\S\ref{sec:setup-lv}), the $T^{n}$ factors are absorbed
into the $\sigma_n$ symbols and the expansion reads $\siv \approx
\sigma_0 + \sigma_1 + \sigma_2 + \cdots$. The three baselines of this
paper are the successive truncations:
\textbf{BBF0} $=\sigma_0$,
\textbf{PHL1} $=\sigma_0+\sigma_1$,
\textbf{GHLOW2} $=\sigma_0+\sigma_1+\sigma_2$.
The explicit closed forms for each $\sigma_n$ follow.

The leading-order BBF map
\citep{berestycki2002} is the inverse harmonic mean
\begin{equation}
\sigma_0(k)\equiv\ivhm(k)=k\Big/\!\int_0^k \frac{dy}{\sloc(y)},
\qquad \sigma_0(0)=\sloc(0),
\end{equation}
whose use as a smile parametrization has practitioner precedent in Hagan
and Woodward \citep{haganWoodward1999}, who fit market smiles through an
equivalent constant-vol approximation; the structural properties of the
harmonic-mean representation itself are characterized in detail by
De~Marco \citep{demarco2021}.
The next-to-leading heat-kernel expansion of Henry-Labord\`ere
\citep{henrylabordere2008} adds a $\sigma_1$ term, giving
$\siv\approx\sigma_0+\sigma_1$. I use the explicit per-strike closed form of
that correction as stated by Gatheral, Hsu, Laurence, Ouyang and Wang
\citep[Thm.~2.4]{gatheral2012}, and keep the conventional label
\textbf{PHL1} for it:
\begin{equation}
\sigma_1(k)=\frac{\sigma_0(k)^3}{2k^2}
  \log\!\frac{\sloc(0)\,\sloc(k)}{\sigma_0(k)^2} .
\label{eq:phl1}
\end{equation}
(Henry-Labord\`ere's own local-to-implied first-order result is the same
correction in the equivalent Hagan--Woodward \citep{haganWoodward1999}
midpoint form.) The \textbf{GHLOW2} refinement adds a further $\sigma_2
T^2$ term from the same source \citep[eq.~3.19, time-homogeneous,
$r=0$]{gatheral2012}:
\begin{equation}
\sigma_2(k)
= -\frac{3\sigma_1}{d^{2}}
  + \frac{3\sigma_1^{2}}{2\sigma_0}
  + \frac{\xi^{3}}{8 d^{5}}
  + \frac{\xi\,(u_1/u_0)}{d^{3}},
\qquad \xi=-k,\quad d=\xi/\sigma_0,
\label{eq:sig2}
\end{equation}
with $u_1/u_0$ the Yoshida heat-kernel ratio (the second-order term in
the small-$T$ expansion of the transition density's leading
coefficient). BBF0 is a harmonic mean of $\sloc$: a $C^2$ knot only
jumps $\sloc'''$, so the integrand $1/\sloc$ stays $C^2$ and $\sigma_0$ is
smooth across the knot --- BBF0 has no knot-specific error, only the generic
leading-order gap present everywhere. The $\sigma_1$ expansion underlying
PHL1 (and hence GHLOW2) is, by contrast, derived assuming $\sloc\in C^3$; at
a $C^2$-only knot it leaves a localized residual --- exactly the term
this paper supplies.

\section{The at-the-money knot correction}

Write $\Delta\siv(k) \equiv \siv(k;\delta) - \siv(k;\delta{=}0)$ for the
implied-vol response to the cubic-coefficient jump $\delta$ in
\eqref{eq:perturb}, relative to the knotless case. Expanding in
$\delta$, at first order, in dimensionless strike $x=k/\sigma$,
\begin{equation}
\Delta\siv(k) \;=\; \delta\cdot\sigma^{3}\cdot K_1(x,0)
\;+\;O(\delta^2).
\label{eq:expansion}
\end{equation}

I call $K_1$ the \emph{first-order Duhamel kernel} for the
local-volatility perturbation: the first-order term of the Dyson series
for the perturbed call-price evolution against constant-$\sigma$ Black.
The bridge form derived below is that Duhamel integral after a
$\lambda=\tau/T$ rescaling exposes the conditional law of the
intermediate position as a standard Brownian bridge; the bridge framing
is bookkeeping for the small-$T$ scaling, not separate machinery. The
universal directed kernel and the PHL1c method that uses it are
developed in \S\ref{sec:universal}; the extension piece and the
GHLOW2cc method are in \S\ref{sec:extension}.

\subsection{The universal kernel and PHL1c}\label{sec:universal}

\paragraph{Very simple derivation.}
First order in $\delta$, the source is $v(y)=2\sigma\,\delta\,y^3\Hev(y)$.
Inserting it once into the Duhamel expansion of the Dupire call price
against the constant-$\sigma$ Black heat kernel, the Black strike-gamma
identity cancels the explicit $\sigma$, and a Gaussian completion turns the
spatial integral into an expectation under a Brownian bridge pinned at
$(0,0)$ and $(k,T)$: writing $\lambda=t/T$, the bridge has
$y\sim\mathcal N(\lambda k,\;\sigma^2\lambda(1-\lambda))$. The remaining
$y$-integral against $y^3\Hev(y)$ is a truncated third moment of a normal,
\begin{equation}
f(\eta)=(\eta^3+3\eta)\,\Phi(\eta)+(\eta^2+2)\,\phi(\eta),
\end{equation}
with $\Phi,\phi$ the standard normal cdf/pdf. The $\sqrt\lambda$ from the
Gaussian completion cancels the $1/\sqrt t$ of the strike-gamma, the outer
time integral collapses to a clean bridge-time integral, and dividing by
the Black vega gives \eqref{eq:expansion} with
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;K_1(x,0)=\int_0^1\big(\lambda(1-\lambda)\big)^{3/2}
   f\big(\eta(\lambda;x)\big)\,d\lambda,\qquad
   \eta(\lambda;x)=x\sqrt{\tfrac{\lambda}{1-\lambda}}.\;}
\label{eq:K1}
\end{equation}
(Full derivation in Appendix~\ref{app:K1}.)
At the knot center $\eta\equiv0$, $f(0)=2/\sqrt{2\pi}$, and the integral is
a Beta function:
\begin{equation}
K_1(0,0)=\frac{2}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\,B\!\left(\tfrac52,\tfrac52\right)
=\frac{3\sqrt{2\pi}}{128}\approx 0.05875
\end{equation}
(Beta-function reduction in Appendix~\ref{app:K1-ATM}).

\paragraph{Why at-the-money.} The general knot at offset
$w \equiv k_{\mathrm{knot}}/\sigma$ keeps a factor $\eta=[\lambda x-(1-\lambda)w]/
\sqrt{\lambda(1-\lambda)}$; the subtraction below then leaves a polynomial
that grows without bound and needs an ad-hoc truncation to stay finite.
Setting $w=0$ removes the $(1-\lambda)w$ term entirely and the correction is
bounded with no hack --- the reason this paper restricts to the ATM knot.

\paragraph{Avoiding double counting.} PHL1 already moves under
\eqref{eq:perturb} through its own $\sigma_0$ and $\sigma_1$. Taylor-expanding
\eqref{eq:phl1} in $\delta$ at $w=0$ gives, for $x>0$,
$\sigma_0$ varies as $x^3/4$ and $\sigma_1$ as $x/4$ (both zero for $x\le0$).
Subtracting these so they are not counted twice yields the \emph{directed}
kernel, which decays on both sides:
\begin{equation}
K_1^{\mathrm{dir}}(x,0)=
\begin{cases}
K_1(x,0)-\dfrac{x^3}{4}-\dfrac{x}{4}, & x>0,\\[2ex]
K_1(x,0), & x\le 0.
\end{cases}
\label{eq:K1-dir}
\end{equation}
I will refer to \eqref{eq:K1-dir} as the \emph{universal} directed
kernel: it is a pure function of $x=k/\sigma$, identical for every
cubic. \S\ref{sec:extension} introduces a second,
$(\sigma,\beta,\alpha,\gamma)$-parametric closed-form piece --- the
\emph{extension piece}, or just the \emph{extension} --- to absorb a
$\sigma_2$-only discontinuity that the universal kernel cannot reach.
These define the three closed-form knot corrections: PHL1c (PHL1
baseline + universal kernel), GHLOW2c (GHLOW2 baseline + universal
kernel), and GHLOW2cc (GHLOW2c + extension).
\paragraph{The correction symbol.} Following the $\sigma_1, \sigma_2$
convention where each closed-form contribution to $\siv$ is a named
quantity, write
\begin{equation}
R^{(3, 1)}_{1}(x, w) \;\equiv\; \delta\cdot\sigma^{3}\cdot K_1^{\mathrm{dir}}(x, w),
\qquad
R^{(3, 1)}_{2} \;\equiv\; R^{(3, 1)}_{1} + (\text{extension piece, }\eqref{eq:extension}),
\label{eq:R-def}
\end{equation}
with $(x, w) = (k/\sigma, k_{\mathrm{knot}}/\sigma)$ inherited from
$K_1$'s arguments, and the extension piece defined in
\S\ref{sec:extension}. The two
superscripts are the constants of this paper: $3$ is the
$\sigma_{\mathrm{loc}}'''$ jump level, and $1$ is the Duhamel-expansion
order (first order in $\delta$); both are fixed throughout, so I
abbreviate $R^{(3, 1)}_{n}$ to $R_{n}$ in the rest of the paper. The
lone subscript $n$ is what varies between the two corrections --- the
baseline T-order whose $\delta$-variations are subtracted from the
bridge integral $K_1$ to give the kernel. The ATM restriction $w=0$
is visible at every callsite. Costeanu and Pirjol's universal
coefficient \citep{costeanuPirjol2011} sits in the same family at
$R^{(1, 1)}_{0}(0, 0)$ (full-family notation needed for the
cross-paper comparison).

The corrected PHL1 method, with PHL1 evaluated on the perturbed surface
so the cancellation is exact to first order, is
\begin{equation}
\siv^{\mathrm{PHL1c}}(k)=\siv^{\mathrm{PHL1}}(k)+R_{1}(k/\sigma, 0).
\end{equation}
I use the suffix ``c'' (lower-case) throughout for ``one closed-form
knot-correction layer applied to this baseline'': PHL1c and GHLOW2c
each carry one such layer (the universal kernel), while GHLOW2cc
carries two (universal kernel + the extension piece), one $c$
per layer.
The bridge integral \eqref{eq:K1} is evaluated with a 32-point
Gauss--Legendre rule.

\subsection{Extension to the GHLOW2 baseline}\label{sec:extension}

The same universal directed kernel \eqref{eq:K1-dir} stacks on the
GHLOW2 baseline to define
\begin{equation}
\siv^{\mathrm{GHLOW2c}}(k)=\siv^{\mathrm{GHLOW2}}(k)+R_{1}(k/\sigma, 0),
\label{eq:ghlow2c}
\end{equation}
equivalently $\siv^{\mathrm{GHLOW2c}}=\siv^{\mathrm{PHL1c}}+\sigma_2$
in total-vol form. Off the knot GHLOW2c is closer to the true Dupire
IV than PHL1c by the smooth $\sigma_2$ term PHL1 omits. At the knot
itself, however, the GHLOW2 baseline carries one more $\delta$-variation
than PHL1 does --- $\sigma_2$'s --- which the universal kernel does not
subtract, leaving an analytic value jump at $k=0$. The
\emph{extension piece} introduced next closes that jump, yielding
GHLOW2cc.

\paragraph{The $\sigma_2$ value jump and its repair.}
The universal directed kernel \eqref{eq:K1-dir} subtracts only BBF0's
and $\sigma_1$'s $\delta$-variations from $K_1$. GHLOW2 carries one
more $\delta$-variation, $\sigma_2$'s, and $\sigma_2(0)$ depends on
$\sloc'''(0)=6\gamma$ via the second-order Yoshida coefficient. The
$u_1/u_0$ integrand carries $\sloc'''$ through its first variation, and
the constraint that makes $\sigma_2(0)$ finite leaves a
$\gamma$-linear residual at the next order. Crucially, every Yoshida
ingredient at $k=0$ is algebraic in $(\sigma,\beta,\alpha,\gamma)$ ---
the ATM restriction $w=0$ removes the integrals, just as it removed the
diverging polynomial in $K_1$ --- so the jump is a closed scalar.
The $\gamma$-linear piece of $\sigma_2(0)$ is $\sigma^{3}\beta\gamma/20$,
so $\gamma$ jumping by $\delta$ at the knot produces a GHLOW2 (and
GHLOW2c) value discontinuity
\begin{equation}
\Delta\sigma_2(0)
= \frac{\sigma^{3}\beta\,\delta}{20},
\label{eq:ghlow2-gap}
\end{equation}
which for the SPXW 1DTE preset is $-0.171$~bps. Subtracting that
$\sigma_2$ $\delta$-variation, clamped between $0$ and $\Delta\sigma_2(0)$,
defines the \emph{extension piece} in total-vol units:
\begin{equation}
R_{2}(k/\sigma, 0) - R_{1}(k/\sigma, 0)
= -\,\mathrm{clamp}\!\left(
   \sigma_2(k;\gamma+\delta) - \sigma_2(k;\gamma);\;
   0,\,\Delta\sigma_2(0)
 \right)\Hev(k),
\label{eq:extension}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{clamp}(z;\,a,b)$ denotes $z$ clipped to the closed
interval between $a$ and $b$ (orientation chosen by their signs).
The extension piece is $(\sigma,\beta,\alpha,\gamma)$-parametric and
lives only on $k>0$ (one-sided perturbation); the clamp forces it to
zero on the wings.
This makes the order content of the extension explicit: it is the
clamped negative of $\sigma_2$'s $\delta$-variation, hence a $T^{2}$
object in the heat-kernel ladder. At $k=0^{+}$ the clamp is inactive
and it takes the value $-\Delta\sigma_2(0)$, exactly cancelling the
GHLOW2c value jump; past the clamp engagement it pins to zero, so on
the wings GHLOW2cc coincides with GHLOW2c. Decomposing $R_{2}$ into
the kernel piece $R_{1}$ ($T^{3/2}$ slope-kink) and the extension
piece $R_{2} - R_{1}$ ($T^{2}$ value-jump) gives the two ``knot
correction'' orders listed for GHLOW2cc in
Table~\ref{tab:summary}.
The fully-corrected GHLOW2 method is then
\begin{equation}
\siv^{\mathrm{GHLOW2cc}}(k)=\siv^{\mathrm{GHLOW2}}(k)+R_{2}(k/\sigma, 0),
\label{eq:ghlow2cc}
\end{equation}
with GHLOW2 again evaluated on the perturbed surface so the cancellation
is exact to first order. The new $\sigma_2$ subtraction is no longer a
universal $x$-only function as the PHL1 kernel's was; it carries the
unperturbed cubic's $(\beta,\alpha,\gamma)$ parametrically, with the
$x=0$ value matching \eqref{eq:ghlow2-gap} exactly. The $w=0$ collapse
that made the bridge integral bounded does \emph{not} extend to the
$\sigma_2$ piece: $\sigma_2$ has a $\xi^{3}/(8 d^{5})$ term with
$d=O(1)$ on the wings, so the raw $\sigma_2$ $\delta$-variation grows
like $k^{3}$. The clamp in \eqref{eq:extension} is what bounds the
construction. At $k=0^{+}$ the raw value equals exactly the cap
$\Delta\sigma_2(0)$ so the clamp is inactive and the value jump is
closed; past the engagement point ($x\approx 1$ for the SPXW preset)
the bare piece reverses sign and the clamp pins it at $0$, so the
extension piece vanishes on the wings and GHLOW2cc collapses to
GHLOW2c there. The clamp is $C^{0}$ but not $C^{1}$ at engagement,
outside the option-meaningful band. The zero-side bound (rather than
the symmetric $\pm|\Delta\sigma_2(0)|$) is what guarantees the wing
collapse.

Figure~\ref{fig:kernel} plots the two correction pieces separately on
the knot example used later in \S\ref{sec:results-knot}. The
\emph{universal} piece $R_{1} =
\delta\,\sigma^{3}\,K_1^{\mathrm{dir}}$ is exactly the gap PHL1c adds
to PHL1 \emph{and} the gap GHLOW2c adds to GHLOW2 (both identical,
sharing the $K_1(0,0)=3\sqrt{2\pi}/128$ peak and decaying on both
sides). The \emph{extension piece} $R_{2} - R_{1}$ is what
GHLOW2cc adds on top of GHLOW2c: it lives only on $k>0$ (one-sided
perturbation), starts at the closed-form ATM scalar
$|\Delta\sigma_2(0)| = 0.171$~bps that closes the $\sigma_2(0)$ value
jump, and decays to zero on the wings as the clip engages.

\paragraph{Why the correction is one-sided.}
At every order in the small-$T$ polynomial expansion (BBF0, PHL1,
GHLOW2, $K_1$), the correction for a strike $k$ depends on the
local-vol surface only along the Varadhan-style geodesic between the
forward and the strike, i.e.\ inside $[\min(0,k),\max(0,k)]$. The
perturbation $\delta k^{3}H(k)$ lives only in $k>0$, so for a strike
$k<0$ the geodesic sits entirely in the untouched region and the
correction is rigorously zero. A Brownian bridge from $0$ to $k<0$
\emph{can} fluctuate into $k>0$, but the probability of that excursion
is $O(\exp(-c/T))$ --- non-perturbative, below every polynomial-in-$T$
term in the expansion. The Dupire PDE captures the exponentially small
cross-side leakage; the closed-form maps do not, by design.

\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

Parameters come from real SPXW surfaces calibrated with a custom
model, then reduced to the form this paper studies: a cubic, or a
cubic with a knot. The Dupire forward PDE (Rannacher start then
Crank--Nicolson) is the ground truth.

\subsection{Smooth cases (no knot)}\label{sec:results-smooth}

The smooth case is the baseline: the cubic local vol with no knot
($\delta=0$), mapped through BBF0, PHL1, and GHLOW2. The corrected
methods PHL1c, GHLOW2c, GHLOW2cc are inert at $\delta=0$ and
reduce to their baselines, so only the three baselines are plotted.
The only error each map carries here is the generic leading-order
gap of its truncation in the heat-kernel ladder --- no slope kink
and no $\sigma_2$ value jump for the knot corrections to repair.
The contrast across the three baselines is purely the order in $T$.

\paragraph{Happy case (Figure~\ref{fig:happy}).} SPXW 2025-03-10,
1DTE. Error ladder (max $|\mathrm{err}|$ in bps over the smile band):
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{BBF0}: $\sim$67~bps. The leading-order gap that
motivates going beyond BBF0 in the first place.
The gap is small enough to be barely visible on the smile panel,
but dominates the middle error panel.
The acceptability depends on the use case:
it captures the shape of the smile, but the error is much more than
PHL1's few bps.
\item \textbf{PHL1}: a few bps. The $\sigma_1$ correction closes
the bulk of the gap; BBF0$\to$PHL1 is the order-of-magnitude step.
\item \textbf{GHLOW2}: a few bps, slightly tighter than PHL1.
The $\sigma_2$ correction sharpens further but the marginal gain
over PHL1 is small compared to the BBF0$\to$PHL1 step.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}

A piecewise-cubic local vol, mapped through PHL1, develops a
localized implied-vol residual at every knot. The cause is a
regularity mismatch: PHL1's $\sigma_1$ expansion assumes $\sloc \in
C^3$, while a piecewise-cubic is only $C^2$ at its knots (the cubic
coefficient $\sloc'''$ jumps there). BBF0, a harmonic-mean integral,
sidesteps the defect by construction. For a knot pinned at the
forward, this paper closes the residual in closed form: the
first-order Duhamel kernel $K_1(x,0)$, a one-dimensional bridge
integral with parameter-free peak $K_1(0,0) = 3\sqrt{2\pi}/128$. The
ATM restriction is essential --- off the forward the same construction
diverges, and the $w=0$ collapse is what fixes the kernel by a single
Beta-function moment. On parameters calibrated from real SPXW
surfaces, the corrected PHL1c tracks the Dupire PDE where PHL1 alone
is biased, and the correction is inert on smooth smiles. The peak
$K_1(0,0)$ is the $C^{2}$ companion in log-normal Black IV to the
$C^{0}$ normal-IV constant $\tfrac{1}{16}\sqrt{\pi/2}$ of Costeanu
and Pirjol --- adjacent slots of the same Duhamel-perturbation
framework they used.

The practical takeaway is that
``piecewise-cubic-with-ATM-knot, mapped through PHL1'' --- i.e.\
PHL1c --- is a usable surface parametrization: it adds the
calibration flexibility a single smooth cubic lacks. (The same
construction lifts to the $T^{2}$ baseline as GHLOW2c/GHLOW2cc, with
a $(\sigma,\beta,\alpha,\gamma)$-parametric piece needed for the
$\sigma_2$ value jump at the knot; the math is less clean and the
marginal gain over PHL1c is small, but the construction is recorded
for completeness.)

Pushing PHL1c into a full production surface parametrization needs
three extensions, none addressed here:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Off-the-forward knots} ($w \equiv \frac{k_{\mathrm{knot}}}{\sigma}
\neq 0$). Off the forward the polynomial in $K_1$ diverges instead of
collapsing to a Beta-function moment, and the construction needs an
ad-hoc truncation to stay finite.
\item \textbf{Knot additivity}. A realistic piecewise-cubic carries
multiple knots. At first order in the per-knot jumps the Duhamel
kernel adds linearly --- each knot contributes independently --- but
second-order interactions between knots, and the regime of validity
of per-knot summation, are not worked out here.
\item \textbf{Wing extrapolation}. Production models stitch the
cubic to a function that has proper wing behavior; analyzing these
additional knots is not worked out here.
\end{itemize}

Until these extensions are worked out, BBF remains the practical
default for a full surface parametrization: it is insensitive to
knot regularity, multi-knot by construction, and trivially extends
to the wings.

\section{First-order Duhamel kernel}\label{app:K1}

Working in log-moneyness $k=\log(K/F)$, $F=1$, $r=0$, the
undiscounted call price $C(T,k)$ obeys the Dupire forward equation
\begin{equation}
\partial_T C
= \tfrac{1}{2}\sloc^2(k)\,(\partial_{kk}-\partial_k)\,C.
\label{eq:dupire-app}
\end{equation}
Split $\sloc^2(k)=\sigma^2 + v(k)$ around the ATM baseline
$\sigma=\sloc(0)$. To first order in $\delta$, the perturbed
local variance is
\[
\sloc^2(k) = (\sigma + \delta\,k^3\Hev(k))^2
= \sigma^2 + 2\sigma\,\delta\,k^3\Hev(k) + O(\delta^2),
\qquad v(k)=2\sigma\,\delta\,k^3\Hev(k).
\]
Decompose $C = C_0 + C_1 + O(\delta^2)$, where $C_0$ is the constant-$\sigma$
Black price and $C_1$ satisfies
\[
\partial_T C_1 - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2(\partial_{kk}-\partial_k)\,C_1
= \tfrac{1}{2}v(k)\,(\partial_{kk}-\partial_k)\,C_0,
\qquad C_1(0,k)=0.
\]
Duhamel's principle gives
\begin{equation}
C_1(T,k)
= \tfrac{1}{2}\int_0^T\!\!dt \int dy\;
   G_\sigma(T-t;\,k,y)\,v(y)\,\Gamma_0(t;y),
\label{eq:duhamel}
\end{equation}
with $G_\sigma$ the constant-$\sigma$ Black propagator and
$\Gamma_0(t;y) = (\partial_{kk}-\partial_k)C_0(t;y)$ the Black
strike-gamma. The Breeden--Litzenberger identity makes
$\Gamma_0(t;y) = \rho_\sigma(y;t)$, the lognormal transition density
of $\log S_t/F$. Both $G_\sigma$ and $\rho_\sigma$ are Gaussian, and
their product--viewed jointly in $(Y(t),Y(T))=(y,k)$--is the
two-point Gaussian law of a Brownian bridge pinned at
$(Y(0),Y(T))=(0,k)$.

Substituting $\lambda=t/T$, the bridge marginal at time $\lambda T$ is
\[
Y(\lambda T)\,\big|\,Y(0)=0,\,Y(T)=k
\;\sim\; \mathcal N\bigl(\lambda k,\;\sigma^2\lambda(1-\lambda)T\bigr).
\]
The spatial integral against $y^3\Hev(y)$ is the truncated third
moment of this Gaussian. Writing $Z\sim\mathcal N(0,1)$ and shifting
to centered coordinates $y = \lambda k + \sigma\sqrt{\lambda(1-\lambda)T}\,Z$,
\[
\mathbb E\bigl[Y(\lambda T)^3\,\Hev(Y(\lambda T))\bigr]
= \bigl(\sigma^2\lambda(1-\lambda)T\bigr)^{3/2}\,f(\eta),
\qquad
f(\eta) = (\eta^3+3\eta)\,\Phi(\eta) + (\eta^2+2)\,\phi(\eta),
\]
with the dimensionless shift $\eta(\lambda;k) = \lambda
k/\sqrt{\sigma^2\lambda(1-\lambda)T}$. ($f$ is the standard
truncated-third-moment formula
$\mathbb E[(\mu+\eta\nu)^3\Hev(\mu+\eta\nu)]/\nu^3$ specialized at
unit variance, $\mu=\eta$.) The $1/\sqrt{t}$ in $\rho_\sigma$
cancels the leading $\sqrt{\lambda}$ in the bridge variance, the
outer time integral collapses to $T\,d\lambda$, and dividing
\eqref{eq:duhamel} by the Black vega $\partial_{\sigma_{\mathrm{BS}}} C_0$
converts the price perturbation to an implied-vol perturbation. With
the $T=1$ convention and $x=k/\sigma$,
$\eta(\lambda;x) = x\sqrt{\lambda/(1-\lambda)}$, and
\begin{equation}
\Delta\siv(k) = \delta\,\sigma^3\,K_1(x,0) + O(\delta^2),
\qquad
K_1(x,0) = \int_0^1\!\bigl(\lambda(1-\lambda)\bigr)^{3/2}\,
            f\bigl(\eta(\lambda;x)\bigr)\,d\lambda.
\label{eq:K1-app}
\end{equation}
This is \eqref{eq:expansion}--\eqref{eq:K1}.

\section{Closed-form peak \texorpdfstring{$K_1(0,0)=3\sqrt{2\pi}/128$}{K1(0,0) = 3 sqrt(2 pi)/128}}\label{app:K1-ATM}

At $x=0$, $\eta(\lambda;0)\equiv0$, so $f(0) = (0)\Phi(0) +
(0+2)\phi(0) = 2/\sqrt{2\pi}$. The bridge integral collapses to a
Beta function:
\[
K_1(0,0) = \frac{2}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_0^1\!\bigl(\lambda(1-\lambda)\bigr)^{3/2}d\lambda
= \frac{2}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\,B\!\bigl(\tfrac52,\tfrac52\bigr),
\]
with $B(a,b) = \Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)/\Gamma(a+b)$. Using
$\Gamma(5/2) = \tfrac34\sqrt{\pi}$ and $\Gamma(5)=24$,
\[
B\!\bigl(\tfrac52,\tfrac52\bigr)
= \frac{(\tfrac34\sqrt\pi)^2}{24}
= \frac{9\pi/16}{24}
= \frac{3\pi}{128},
\]
and so
\[
K_1(0,0) = \frac{2}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\cdot\frac{3\pi}{128}
= \frac{6\pi}{128\sqrt{2\pi}}
= \frac{3\sqrt{2\pi}}{128}
\approx 0.05875.
\]

\section{\texorpdfstring{$\delta$-variation of BBF0 and PHL1 at $w=0$}{delta-variation of BBF0 and PHL1 at w=0}}\label{app:delta-var}

This justifies the $x^3/4 + x/4$ subtraction in the universal directed
kernel \eqref{eq:K1-dir}. Constant-$\sigma$ baseline
$\sloc^0\equiv\sigma$, ATM-knot perturbation
$\sloc(k) = \sigma + \delta\,k^3\Hev(k)$.

\paragraph{BBF0.} For $k>0$, $1/\sloc(y) = 1/\sigma - \delta
y^3/\sigma^2 + O(\delta^2)$, so
\[
\int_0^k\!\frac{dy}{\sloc(y)}
= \frac{k}{\sigma} - \frac{\delta\,k^4}{4\sigma^2} + O(\delta^2),
\qquad
\sigma_0(k) = \frac{k}{\int_0^k dy/\sloc(y)}
= \sigma + \frac{\delta\,k^3}{4} + O(\delta^2).
\]
(For $k\le0$ the perturbation has no support and $\sigma_0=\sigma$
exactly.) Dividing the first-order coefficient $k^3/4$ by $\sigma^3$
and writing $x=k/\sigma$ gives the kernel-form subtraction
$x^3/4\cdot\Hev(x)$.

\paragraph{PHL1.} Use eq.~\eqref{eq:phl1}, $\sigma_1(k) = \sigma_0(k)^3/(2k^2)
\cdot \log\bigl(\sloc(0)\sloc(k)/\sigma_0(k)^2\bigr)$. With
$\sloc(0)=\sigma$, $\sloc(k)=\sigma+\delta k^3$,
$\sigma_0(k)=\sigma+\delta k^3/4$, the log argument is
\[
\frac{\sigma\,(\sigma+\delta k^3)}{(\sigma+\delta k^3/4)^2}
= \frac{1 + \delta k^3/\sigma}{1 + \delta k^3/(2\sigma) + O(\delta^2)}
= 1 + \frac{\delta\,k^3}{2\sigma} + O(\delta^2),
\]
so $\log(\cdot) = \delta k^3/(2\sigma) + O(\delta^2)$. The prefactor
$\sigma_0(k)^3/(2k^2) = \sigma^3/(2k^2) + O(\delta)$, giving
\[
\sigma_1(k) = \frac{\sigma^3}{2k^2}\cdot\frac{\delta\,k^3}{2\sigma} + O(\delta^2)
= \frac{\delta\,\sigma^2\,k}{4} + O(\delta^2).
\]
Dividing by $\sigma^3$ and writing $x=k/\sigma$, the kernel-form
subtraction is $x/4\cdot\Hev(x)$.

The two pieces together are the BBF0 and $\sigma_1$
$\delta$-variations that the universal directed kernel
\eqref{eq:K1-dir} subtracts from $K_1$ so that adding $R_{1}$
to PHL1 does not double-count the variation PHL1 already captures.

\section{The \texorpdfstring{$\sigma_2(0)$}{sigma\_2(0)} value jump scalar}\label{app:sig2-jump}

The closed form $\Delta\sigma_2(0) = \sigma^3\beta\delta/20$
\eqref{eq:ghlow2-gap} follows from the structure of
\eqref{eq:sig2} at $k=0$. Write the bracket
$B(k) = -3\sigma_1(k)\,\sigma_0(k)^2 + \sigma_0(k)^5/8
+ (u_1/u_0)(k)\,\sigma_0(k)^3$
so that \eqref{eq:sig2} rearranges into
\[
\sigma_2(k) = \frac{B(k)}{k^2}
\;+\; \frac{3\sigma_1(k)^2}{2\sigma_0(k)}.
\]
For $\sigma_2(0)$ to be finite, $B(0)=B'(0)=0$, which fixes the
$\gamma$-free part of $u_1/u_0$ at $k=0$ and makes the
$\gamma$-cancellation at first $k$-derivative explicit
($\sigma_1'(0)|_\gamma$ contributes $-\tfrac34\,\sigma^4\gamma$ to $B'(0)$
and $(u_1/u_0)'(0)|_\gamma$ contributes $+\tfrac34\,\sigma^4\gamma$).
The $\gamma$-bearing piece survives at second order in $k$:
\[
B''(0)\big|_\gamma = \frac{\sigma^3\beta\gamma}{10},
\]
and L'H\^opital twice gives
\[
\sigma_2(0)\big|_\gamma = \frac{B''(0)|_\gamma}{2}
= \frac{\sigma^3\beta\gamma}{20}.
\]
So $\sigma_2(0)$ depends on $\gamma$ exactly linearly. Replacing
$\gamma$ by $\gamma+\delta$ at the knot ($k=0^+$) flips the
$\gamma$-coefficient by $\delta$, producing the value jump
\[
\Delta\sigma_2(0) = \frac{\sigma^3\beta\,\delta}{20},
\]
which is \eqref{eq:ghlow2-gap}. The ATM restriction $w=0$ is what
keeps every ingredient algebraic in $(\sigma,\beta,\alpha,\gamma)$
and the jump scalar closed-form.
